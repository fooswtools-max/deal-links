  • Active funds charge 0.50–1.00% in fees vs 0.03–0.10% for index funds
  • The fee difference alone costs the average investor $200,000+ over a 30-year career
  • Mid-cap and small-cap active funds have even higher underperformance rates (95% and 93%)

Why Index Funds Win Over Time

The data is overwhelming: the longer the time period, the worse active managers perform relative to their benchmarks. This is not because fund managers lack skill — it's because fees, trading costs, and cash drag compound over time. An active fund charging 0.75% annually must outperform by that amount just to match an index fund. Over 20 years, that’s a massive hurdle.

The Fee Impact: A Real-Dollar Example

Consider two investors each putting $500/month into the market for 30 years. One uses an S&P 500 index fund (0.03% fee), the other an active large-cap fund (0.75% fee). Assuming both earn 10% gross returns, the index fund investor ends with $987,000 while the active fund investor ends with $838,000 — a $149,000 difference from fees alone. If the active fund also underperforms (as 92% do), the gap widens dramatically.
