Why Inflation Matters for Investors

A dollar today buys less than a dollar yesterday. Since 1928, consumer prices have risen an average of 3.1% per year. This means an investment earning 10% nominally really only grows your purchasing power by about 7%. During the stagflation of the 1970s, high inflation (7.4% annually) actually wiped out stock market gains entirely, delivering negative real returns for the full decade.

Stocks vs Bonds vs Cash: The Inflation Race

Over the long term, stocks have been the best inflation hedge. Real stock returns average 7.0%, bonds about 2.0%, and cash (Treasury bills) about 0.4%. A dollar held in cash since 1928 would have lost about 95% of its purchasing power. That same dollar invested in the S&P 500 would have grown to over $600 in real terms. The message: not investing is the biggest risk of all.
