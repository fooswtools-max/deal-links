Key Statistics at a Glance

  • The S&P 500 has averaged 10.3% annual returns since 1928 (about 7.0% after inflation)
  • The market has been positive in roughly 73% of calendar years
  • The worst single year was 1931 at -43.8%; the best was 1954 at +52.6%
  • Even including crashes, a $10,000 investment in 1980 would be worth over $1,100,000 today
  • The 2010s decade delivered 13.6% annualized returns — the second-best decade on record

What the Long-Term Data Shows

Over nearly a century of data, the stock market has rewarded patient investors. Despite two world wars, the Great Depression, the dot-com bust, the 2008 financial crisis, and a global pandemic, the S&P 500 has delivered roughly 10% annualized returns. Adjusted for inflation, real returns average about 7% per year. The key insight: time in the market consistently beats timing the market.

Decade-by-Decade Performance

Market returns vary dramatically by decade. The 1950s and 1990s were exceptional (19.4% and 18.2% respectively), while the 1930s and 2000s were essentially flat. Investors who stayed invested through the "lost decade" of the 2000s were rewarded with 13.6% annualized returns in the 2010s. This pattern of recovery after poor decades has repeated throughout market history.

The Power of Dividends

Dividends account for roughly 40% of total stock market returns over the long run. From 1930 to 1950, when price returns were minimal, dividend income kept investors in positive territory. In recent decades, dividends have contributed less (about 2% per year) as companies have shifted toward buybacks. But reinvested dividends remain a critical component of compound growth.
