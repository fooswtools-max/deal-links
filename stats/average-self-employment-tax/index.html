Why Self-Employment Tax Feels So High

When you work for an employer, you pay 7.65% for Social Security and Medicare, and your employer matches that amount. Self-employed individuals pay both halves — 15.3% total. On $75,000 of net income, that's $10,597 in SE tax before a single dollar of income tax. This is why many freelancers and gig workers are shocked by their first tax bill. The silver lining: half of SE tax ($5,299 in this example) is deductible, lowering your taxable income.

Strategies to Reduce Self-Employment Tax

The most effective strategy is forming an S-Corporation. S-Corp owners pay themselves a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll tax) and take remaining profits as distributions (not subject to SE tax). A freelancer earning $150,000 who pays themselves a $90,000 salary saves approximately $8,500 in SE tax annually. Other strategies include maximizing retirement contributions (SEP-IRA up to $69,000, Solo 401k) which reduce income tax though not SE tax.
