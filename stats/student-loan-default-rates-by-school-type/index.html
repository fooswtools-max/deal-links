Key Statistics at a Glance

  • For-profit schools have a 13.6% default rate — 3x higher than public universities (4.4%)
  • The overall national 3-year cohort default rate is 7.0%
  • Students who drop out default at 3x the rate of graduates
  • Borrowers with less than $5,000 in debt default more often than those with $50,000+
  • Two-year schools have higher default rates (8.2%) than four-year schools (4.8%)

The Default Paradox: Small Debts, High Default

Counter-intuitively, borrowers with the smallest debts default at the highest rates. Those with under $5,000 default at 18%, while those with $50,000+ default at just 5%. This is because low-debt borrowers often dropped out of school (getting no degree benefit) or attended programs with poor labor market outcomes. High-debt borrowers typically completed graduate degrees and earn enough to make payments.

For-Profit Schools: The Data is Clear

For-profit institutions enroll about 10% of students but account for nearly 40% of all student loan defaults. Their students borrow more, earn less after graduation, and drop out more frequently. The data consistently shows that for-profit attendees have worse financial outcomes than their counterparts at public and nonprofit institutions, even after controlling for student demographics.
