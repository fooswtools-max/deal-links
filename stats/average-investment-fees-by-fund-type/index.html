The Fee Revolution: How Costs Have Plummeted

Investment fees have fallen dramatically over the past two decades. In 2004, the asset-weighted average expense ratio was 0.87%. By 2025, it had dropped to 0.36% — a 59% decline. This is largely thanks to the rise of index funds and ETFs, which now command over 50% of total US fund assets. Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab have driven costs to near zero for basic index products, with several now offering 0.00% expense ratio funds.

The True Cost of a 1% Fee Difference

Small fee differences compound into enormous dollar amounts. An investor putting $500/month into a fund earning 8% gross returns ends up with $1,490,000 after 40 years in a 0.05% index fund, but only $1,146,000 in a 0.75% active fund and just $900,000 in a 1.50% high-fee fund. That 1% difference between the index and active fund is worth $344,000 — nearly 7 years of contributions.
